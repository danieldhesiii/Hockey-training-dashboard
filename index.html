Your System

A complete, physiology-driven plan built around your Saturday game. Everything below works fully offline — save it to your home screen.

How this is built

Hockey is an alactic-aerobic sport: repeated 40–60s max-effort shifts with ~2–3× rest, decided by explosive first strides, shot power, and how fast you recover between bursts. So the plan trains, in priority order:

  • Max strength — the base that makes every stride, hit and shot more forceful.
  • Power & speed — converting that strength into fast, explosive output (jumps, throws, Olympic variations, sprints, contrast work).
  • Energy systems — alactic power + anaerobic capacity for shifts, and a strong aerobic base so you recover between them.
  • Injury-proofing — groin, hamstrings, hips, shoulders, neck and tendons via targeted isometrics & prehab woven into every session.
  • Reactive plyometrics — low-volume, read-and-react fast-twitch drills (including your rear-foot-elevated tap drill) for a faster first step.

The core principle: hard / easy

You train 6 days plus a game — that only works if intensity undulates. Every hard day is followed by something the body can recover through. Heavy CNS work (max strength, power, sprints) sits early-to-mid week after you've recovered from the game; the day before the game is a short primer that leaves you springy, not sore. This is how advanced athletes hold high frequency without burning out.

Your day (built around a 9–5)

Two touch-points a day: a quick morning micro-dose that doesn't cost recovery, then your evening main session once you've switched off from work.

07:00Optional 10–15 min — rehab isometrics + mobility (+ stickhandling on skill days). Greases the joints, primes the day, zero fatigue cost.

If your game moves (Fri instead of Sat)

The sequence is anchored, not fixed to weekdays. Rule of thumb: the day before the game is always the Primer, and the day after is always Recovery/Aerobic. Everything else keeps its order. So if the game shifts to Friday, Thursday becomes the primer, and Saturday becomes your recovery/aerobic day (or an extra strength session — see below).

No game this Saturday?

Swap the Primer + Game for one of these on Sat, and keep Sunday as recovery:

  • Option A — Extra Lower Power: repeat a trimmed version of the Monday lower session (drop 1–2 accessories) to bank another quality strength stimulus.
  • Option B — Long conditioning: a full energy-system session (repeat-sprint + aerobic finisher) to build your engine while there's no game to save for.
  • Option C — Full rest: if the last block was heavy, take the day. Six quality days already beats seven junk ones.

Reactive  Reactive plyometrics & fast-twitch drills

Trains the stretch-shortening cycle and read-and-react speed — your first step, edges and cuts. Rules: do them fresh, low volume, max quality, full recovery. They're programmed up top on your Wednesday & Thursday sessions, and lightly in Friday's primer — never to fatigue.

Your reactive tap drill — single-leg, rear-foot elevated
RFE Reactive Foot-TapsRear foot resting light on a low box behind you (kickstand); weight on the front working leg. Keep a fast, springy in-place pogo tap on the ball of the working foot. On a called cue — "front / left / right" — reach and tap that spot ONCE, then snap straight back to the in-place rhythm. React fast, quiet feet, minimal ground-contact time.3–5 × 10–20s / leg
Progress it: add a 4th direction (back), speed up the calls, catch a ball mid-drill, shift more weight onto the front leg (less kickstand), or reach further. A partner calling — or a shuffled-cue / random-number app — adds the reaction element that mirrors reading the play.
Supporting reactive / fast-twitch drills
Pogo hops → single-leg pogosStiff ankles, straight legs, ground like it's hot. Double-leg first, then single.3×10–15
Single-leg box bounds (ankle stiffness)Bound up onto a low box on one leg; land soft but springy with a stiff, reactive ankle and minimal ground-contact time. Quick, quiet pops — quality over height. Builds the ankle stiffness that transfers to a faster first stride.3×4–5 / leg
Reactive line / dot hopsQuick-feet patterns (fwd-back, side-side, X). Speed of contacts is the point.3×15–20s
Skater bounds (stick landings)Lateral bound, stick & hold 1s, then progress to continuous. Pure hockey edge power.3×4–6 / side
Snap-down / drop-and-stickReactive deceleration — teaches you to absorb force and re-load fast.3×4
Reaction first-step (on cue)React to a call or visual and explode 5m. Pure reaction + acceleration.4–6 reps
Keep contacts low (~40–80 quality foot-contacts total) and fully recover between sets. Reactive work is nervous-system training, not conditioning — if it becomes a gasser, you've lost the point. Depth jumps: Power phase only.
Warm-up (do before every session): 3–5 min bike/row, then dynamic hips (leg swings, adductor rockbacks, world's-greatest-stretch), ankle mobility, glute activation (banded walks), and 2–3 build-up efforts of the day's first main movement. Never load heavy or sprint cold.

Rehab & Prehab

Wrist, knee and shoulder — isometrics first, pain-guided, then progress the load. The schedule shows which day to hit each.

Read first: this is general rehab programming, not a diagnosis. If something hasn't been assessed — or you get numbness/tingling, giving way, night pain, or it's not improving in 2–3 weeks — see a physio or doctor. Sharp pain is a stop sign: work around it, never through it.

🩹 Wrist

The pain rule (every drill)

  • Keep pain ≤ 3/10 during the work, and it should settle to baseline within 24h.
  • Sore the next day = too much. Drop the load/effort next time.
  • Done right, isometrics often reduce pain — use them daily.
Now · Phase 1  Isometric holds — home, daily

No movement — build tolerance and calm the joint by pressing into a fixed resistance (your other hand, a table edge, a wall, a band). Effort ~40–60% to start, pain ≤3/10, 1–2× per day.

Protocol · 4–5 holds × 30–45s · rest ~30s
Iso wrist extensionPalm down — press the back of your hand up into your other hand or under a table.5×30–45s
Iso wrist flexionPalm up — press up into your other hand.5×30–45s
Iso radial / ulnar deviationThumb up (neutral), press sideways into a fixed object each way.4×20–30s ea
Iso supination / pronationHold a light hammer or dumbbell, resist rotation with the other hand. Both ways.4×20–30s ea
Sub-max grip squeezeSqueeze a rolled towel or soft ball at ~50%. Pain-free only.5×20–30s
Start gentler if it's currently irritable (shorter holds, lighter press) and build effort week to week as pain allows.
~2 weeks · Phase 2  Dynamic loading — when it's calmer

Once the isometrics are pain-free and daily use is comfortable, add controlled movement and light load. Slow tempo — especially the lowering. Still pain ≤3/10, still at home.

2–3 sets · 12–15 reps · light & controlled
Wrist flexion curlForearm on thigh, light DB/band, curl up, lower slow (3s).3×12–15
Wrist extension curlPalm-down version — usually the key one. Very light to start.3×12–15
Radial / ulnar deviationHammer or light DB held vertical, tilt up/down.3×12–15
Pronation / supinationHammer or band — rotate palm up ↔ down under control.3×12–15
Rice-bucket workDig, twist and open your hand in a bucket of rice — brilliant low-load forearm endurance.2–3×30–45s
Wrist CARsSlow, full-range controlled circles to restore movement quality.2×5 ea way
Progress load only when there's no flare 24h later. Any flare → drop back to Phase 1 for a few days, then rebuild.

Training around it — gym swaps

Keep hammering the rest of the plan — just spare the wrist:

  • Pulls (deadlift, RDL, rows): use straps — takes grip/wrist out of it.
  • Pressing: favour neutral-grip dumbbells, a football/Swiss bar, or landmine press; add wrist wraps for support.
  • Cleans / catches: the rack catch loads the wrist in extension — use the trap-bar jump or a DB hang high-pull (already your Monday option).
  • Front squat: skip the barbell rack grip — safety-squat bar, cross-arm/strap hold, or heels-elevated goblet.
  • Pull-ups: neutral-grip handles or straps; if it bites, swap to chest-supported row / lat pulldown.
  • Med-ball catches: keep the wrist neutral and firm, or reduce until Phase 2. Foot/lower-body plyos are wrist-safe — lean on those for now.
Lower-body and posterior-chain work barely touch the wrist. Load those hard, use straps/neutral grips up top, and let the isometrics do their job.

🦵 Knee

Tendon pain rule

  • Tendon (patellar) pain up to ≤ 3–4/10 during loading is OK — judge it by next-morning stiffness, not just the session.
  • Worse or stiffer the next morning = too much load; drop it back.
  • Heavy isometrics can calm an angry tendon and even reduce pain — use them on sore days.
Phase 1  Isometrics — calm & load the tendon

Long, heavy-ish holds through a pain-free range. Great before or after training, or daily if it's irritable.

4–5 holds × 30–45s · rest ~1–2 min
Spanish squatBand behind the knees, sit back into a ~70–90° hold, shins vertical. The go-to patellar-tendon iso.5×30–45s
Wall sitThighs parallel, hold. Simple and effective if you've no band.4×30–45s
Iso leg-extension holdMachine or banded — hold at ~60° knee bend, hard but pain ≤3–4.4×30s
Iso split-squat holdLoads one side — hold mid-range, tall torso.3×20–30s / leg
Phase 2  Slow isotonic loading — build tendon capacity

Once isometrics are comfortable, load through range with a slow tempo (3s down, 2–3s up). This is what actually thickens and strengthens the tendon.

3–4 sets · 6–8 reps · slow & controlled
Slow leg extension3s down, pause, drive up. Build load week to week if no morning flare.3–4×6–8
Heels-elevated / decline squatPlate or slant under heels — biases the quad & tendon. Slow descent.3×8
Step-downs (eccentric)Off a low box, lower under control on the working leg. Knee tracks over toe.3×8 / leg
Leg press (slow)Controlled, full but pain-free range — great scalable load.3×8–10
Nordic / RDL (hamstring)Balance the knee with posterior-chain strength.3×6–8
Tibialis + calf raiseBelow-knee resilience — protects the knee on stops & landings.3×12–15
Then re-introduce jumps/plyos only once slow strength is pain-free — start with landings (absorb), then low-amplitude hops, before reactive work.

🫱 Shoulder

How to load it

  • Pain ≤ 3/10, no sharp catching. Cuff & scap work can be done most days at low load.
  • If it's irritable, start with isometrics; add movement as it settles.
  • A robust shoulder and neck is your best insurance against checks and falls.
